# IBM Hills Template

Hills are outcome-driven statements used in IBM Enterprise Design Thinking to align teams around user value. A Hill declares what a user will be able to achieve, stated in terms that are measurable and testable, without prescribing how the solution should work.

## The Hills Format

> **Who** (specific user type) can **What** (task or goal they accomplish) with **Wow** (measurable, differentiating outcome).

Every Hill must answer three questions:
- **Who** is the user? Be specific -- not "users" but "first-time small business owners."
- **What** can they accomplish? Describe the end state, not the feature.
- **Wow** -- what makes it remarkable? Include a measurable outcome that raises the bar.

## Examples Across Domains

### Project Management Tool
> A **project manager overseeing 3+ concurrent projects** can **see every overdue task across all projects** with **zero manual data gathering and in under 10 seconds from login**.

### E-Commerce Checkout
> A **returning customer with items in their cart** can **complete checkout** with **no more than 2 clicks and without re-entering payment or shipping information**.

### Healthcare Patient Portal
> A **patient with a chronic condition** can **view their full medication history and upcoming appointments** with **a single unified timeline that loads in under 3 seconds on a mobile device**.

### Developer Documentation
> A **backend developer integrating our API for the first time** can **make a successful authenticated API call** with **only the information on the quickstart page and within 15 minutes of reading it**.

### Education Platform
> A **high school teacher managing 5 class sections** can **identify students who are falling behind** with **an automated weekly summary that requires no manual grade analysis**.

## How Hills Differ from User Stories

User stories and Hills serve different purposes and operate at different levels of abstraction.

### User Stories

Format: "As a [user], I want [feature], so that [benefit]."

Example: "As a project manager, I want a cross-project dashboard, so that I can see overdue tasks."

User stories describe a **feature request** from the user's perspective. They are implementation-oriented and typically map to a single sprint or ticket. They are useful for backlog management and developer tasking.

### Hills

Format: "Who can What with Wow."

Example: "A project manager overseeing 3+ concurrent projects can see every overdue task across all projects with zero manual data gathering and in under 10 seconds from login."

Hills describe a **desired outcome** without specifying the solution. They are strategy-oriented and typically span multiple sprints or an entire release. They are useful for aligning stakeholders and measuring success.

### Key Differences

| Aspect | User Story | Hill |
|---|---|---|
| Focus | Feature/solution | Outcome/value |
| Specificity | Prescribes what to build | Describes what to achieve |
| Measurability | Acceptance criteria added separately | Wow factor is built-in |
| Scope | Single ticket or sprint | Release or initiative |
| Stakeholder alignment | Developer-focused | Executive and cross-team |

## Writing Good Hills

1. **Start with the user, not the system.** "The dashboard shows..." is wrong. "A project manager can see..." is right.
2. **Make the Wow measurable.** "Easily" is not measurable. "In under 10 seconds" is.
3. **Avoid solution language.** Do not mention specific UI elements, technologies, or implementation details.
4. **Keep it to one sentence.** If you need a paragraph, your Hill is too complex -- break it into two Hills.
5. **Test it with stakeholders.** If a non-technical stakeholder cannot understand the Hill, rewrite it.

## Using Hills in the Define Stage

After generating HMW questions, convert the top 2-3 most promising opportunities into Hills. Each Hill becomes a measurable success criterion that the Prototype and Test stages evaluate against. The Wow factor is especially important because it gives the Test agent concrete metrics for evaluation.
