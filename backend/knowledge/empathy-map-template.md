# Empathy Map Template

An empathy map organizes user research into four quadrants that capture the full picture of a user's experience. It helps the design team step outside their own assumptions and develop genuine understanding of the people they are designing for.

## Structure

### Thinks

What occupies the user's mind during the experience? These are internal thoughts, beliefs, and concerns that they may not say out loud.

Prompts to uncover this quadrant:
- What are their goals and aspirations related to this task?
- What worries or concerns do they have?
- What assumptions or mental models are they carrying?
- What are they trying to figure out?

### Feels

The emotional landscape of the user's experience. Feelings drive behavior more than logic, so this quadrant often reveals the most actionable insights.

Prompts to uncover this quadrant:
- What frustrates them about the current process?
- What delights or motivates them?
- What makes them anxious or uncertain?
- When do they feel confident or accomplished?

### Says

Direct quotes, language patterns, and communication style. These come from interviews, support tickets, social media posts, and survey responses.

Prompts to uncover this quadrant:
- What exact words do they use to describe the problem?
- What do they tell colleagues or friends about this experience?
- What questions do they ask most frequently?
- What complaints or praise do they voice?

### Does

Observable actions, behaviors, and workarounds. This quadrant focuses on what users actually do, which often differs from what they say they do.

Prompts to uncover this quadrant:
- What steps do they take to accomplish their goal?
- What workarounds have they developed?
- Where do they get stuck or hesitate?
- What tools or resources do they turn to?

## Worked Example: Project Manager Using a Task App

**User**: Sarah, 34, Project Manager at a mid-size software company. Manages 3 concurrent projects with teams of 5-8 people each.

### Thinks

- "I need to know which tasks are blocking the sprint before our 9am standup."
- "I can never remember which project uses which labels -- every team has different conventions."
- "If I could see all overdue tasks across all projects in one view, I would save 20 minutes every morning."
- "I worry that tasks assigned to contractors slip through the cracks because they do not update status."

### Feels

- **Frustrated** when switching between three separate project boards to build a status report.
- **Anxious** before stakeholder meetings because she is never sure the board reflects reality.
- **Relieved** when a team member moves a task to Done without being reminded.
- **Overwhelmed** on Mondays when the backlog has 40+ ungroomed items.
- **Proud** when a sprint finishes on time and she can point to the board as the source of truth.

### Says

- "I spend more time updating the board than actually managing the project."
- "Can we get a cross-project view? I have asked three times."
- "The notification system is useless -- I get 50 emails a day and none of them are the ones that matter."
- "I just want to see what is late. That is it."

### Does

- Opens the app first thing in the morning and filters by "Due this week."
- Manually copies overdue tasks into a spreadsheet for the Monday leadership meeting.
- Sends Slack messages to individuals asking for status updates because she does not trust the board.
- Creates personal labels like "SARAH-WATCH" to flag items she needs to track.
- Checks the activity log to verify who actually moved a card versus who just commented.

## Using the Empathy Map

After completing the map:

1. **Identify contradictions** between Says and Does -- these reveal unmet needs.
2. **Cluster emotions** in the Feels quadrant to find the dominant emotional experience.
3. **Extract pain points** from Thinks and Feels for the Define stage.
4. **Derive design principles** -- for Sarah, a principle might be "Surface what matters without requiring manual effort."

The empathy map feeds directly into the POV statement and HMW questions in the Define stage.
